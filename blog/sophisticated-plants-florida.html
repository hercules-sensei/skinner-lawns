The Most Sophisticated Plants for Your Florida Landscape

A truly elegant Florida landscape doesn't require high maintenance — it requires the right plants in the right places. These seven species deliver texture, color, structure, and year-round interest while thriving in Jacksonville's climate without constant intervention.

There's a persistent myth that Florida landscaping means either a wall-to-wall lawn of St. Augustine or a cluttered mix of tropical exotics that demand constant attention. The reality is that Northeast Florida's climate supports some genuinely stunning ornamental plants — plants that bring the kind of refined, layered look you'd see in a high-end botanical garden, and that largely take care of themselves once established. Here are seven that we incorporate into landscape designs regularly at Skinner Lawns.

Blazing Star (Liatris spicata)

Few flowering perennials create a more dramatic vertical statement than Blazing Star, also called Dense Blazing Star or Dense Gayfeather. From late summer through fall, it produces tall, narrow spikes of magenta-purple flowers that bloom from the top down — unusual among spike-flowering plants and particularly effective from a design standpoint. The blooms can reach 3–5 feet tall, making it excellent as a backdrop plant or a specimen in a perennial border.

Blazing Star is native to the eastern United States and performs exceptionally well in Jacksonville's sandy, well-drained soils. It requires full sun and moderate moisture but tolerates drought once established. Use it in clusters of three or five for maximum visual impact along fence lines, at property corners, or mixed into wildflower-style plantings with Black-eyed Susans and native grasses. Butterflies and pollinators find it irresistible, which adds another layer of living interest to the garden.

Care needs: Full sun, well-drained soil, moderate water. Cut back spent flower spikes after bloom. Divide clumps every 3–4 years to maintain vigor.

Fakahatchee Grass (Tripsacum dactyloides)

Fakahatchee Grass — also known as Eastern Gamagrass — is one of Florida's most elegant native ornamental grasses. It forms a large, arching mound of deep green, ribbon-like blades that can reach 4–6 feet in height and spread. Unlike many ornamental grasses that look ragged and brown through winter, Fakahatchee remains lush and attractive through Jacksonville's mild winter months, providing year-round structure.

In the landscape, it functions beautifully as a large-scale accent, a naturalized screen, or a dramatic focal point at the end of a pathway. It adapts well to both moist and moderately dry conditions — making it one of the more versatile choices for North Florida properties where soil moisture varies across the yard. It's also excellent in rain garden applications, helping to absorb and filter stormwater runoff.

Care needs: Full sun to partial shade, adaptable to various soil moisture levels. Cut back to about 6 inches in late February before new growth emerges. Very low maintenance once established.

Gulf Muhly Grass (Muhlenbergia capillaris)

If you've driven through Jacksonville in October and noticed clouds of pink-purple haze billowing from landscape beds — that's Gulf Muhly Grass putting on its annual show. This native grass is perhaps the most spectacular fall-blooming ornamental available to North Florida gardeners. The flower plumes emerge in September and October, creating a diaphanous mist of color that moves beautifully in the coastal breeze. The effect in mass plantings is nothing short of breathtaking.

Gulf Muhly grows in tight, 3-foot mounds of fine, dark green foliage that are attractive even when not in bloom. It thrives in Jacksonville's sandy, slightly acidic soils and is among the most drought-tolerant ornamental grasses for this region. Plant it in full sun in masses of five or more for the full visual effect — single plants are attractive, but groups are transformative.

Care needs: Full sun, well-drained soil, drought-tolerant once established. Cut back to 6–8 inches in late winter. No fertilizer needed in most North Florida soils.

Horsetail Reed (Equisetum hyemale)

Horsetail Reed is one of the most architecturally striking plants available to Jacksonville landscapers. Its hollow, jointed, bright-green stems grow in dense vertical columns, creating a bold graphic element that looks almost sculptural in the landscape. It brings a distinctly contemporary, minimalist aesthetic that pairs beautifully with modern home architecture — clean lines, strong verticals, and zero fussiness.

Horsetail Reed is ideally suited to wet, boggy areas that challenge most plants — think rain garden edges, pond margins, or any low spot in the yard that holds water. It can also be grown in containers to control its spreading habit, making it usable on patios, entry courtyards, and small-space gardens. Be aware: in the ground without containment, it spreads assertively via underground rhizomes, so planting in a buried root barrier or a submerged container is advisable for most residential situations.

Care needs: Full sun to partial shade, moist to wet soil. Nearly indestructible once established. Contain the roots to prevent spreading. Avoid dry, well-drained situations.

Sabal Palm (Sabal palmetto)

The Sabal Palm — Florida's official state tree — is more than a tropical statement piece. It's a structural anchor for any Northeast Florida landscape, providing year-round height, permanence, and that unmistakable sense of place that defines this coastline. A mature Sabal Palm can reach 40–60 feet, but young specimens work in almost any landscape at 8–15 feet.

Unlike many ornamental palms, the Sabal Palm is genuinely cold-hardy — surviving temperatures well below freezing — making it perfectly suited to Jacksonville's occasional winter cold snaps. It tolerates salt spray, drought, flooding, and the occasional hurricane. In the landscape, use single specimens as focal points, or plant in staggered groves of three or five for a naturalistic effect. Pair them with Fakahatchee Grass or Firebush at the base for a layered, native composition.

Care needs: Full sun, tolerates a wide range of soils. Do not over-fertilize. Never trim the green fronds — remove only completely dead, brown fronds. Avoid "hurricane cutting," which stresses the palm and reduces its ability to withstand actual storms.

Lantana (Lantana camara and native species)

Few plants work harder in a Jacksonville landscape than Lantana. It blooms nearly year-round in Northeast Florida's climate, producing dense clusters of flowers in combinations of orange, yellow, red, pink, and white that attract butterflies and hummingbirds in extraordinary numbers. It handles full sun, reflected heat, salt air, and drought with equal indifference — essentially thriving on neglect.

Use Lantana as a mass groundcover on slopes and sunny banks where irrigation is limited, as a colorful edging along driveways and walkways, or trailing over retaining walls. The trailing varieties, particularly Lantana montevidensis in purple, are especially elegant when allowed to cascade over a wall or planter edge. For a more refined look, the dwarf varieties like 'Radiation' and 'Miss Huff' stay compact without frequent shearing.

Care needs: Full sun, well-drained soil, drought-tolerant once established. Cut back hard (to about 6 inches) once or twice a year to maintain shape and encourage flush blooming. Note: the common cultivar L. camara can be invasive in South Florida natural areas — in North Florida the risk is lower, but consider native L. involucrata where ecological sensitivity is a concern.

Black-Eyed Susan (Rudbeckia hirta)

Black-Eyed Susans are a workhorse perennial that punch well above their weight class in the landscape. The classic golden-yellow daisy flowers with dark centers bloom prolifically from late spring through summer and into fall, providing months of color when many other perennials have faded. They naturalize readily in Jacksonville's climate, spreading year after year without replanting.

In a well-designed landscape, Black-Eyed Susans bridge the gap between structured planting beds and naturalistic meadow gardens. They look equally at home in a tidy border alongside Muhly Grass and Blazing Star as they do in a wildflower meadow with Coreopsis and native grasses. They're a critical pollinator plant — dozens of native bee species depend on their late-season pollen as summer flowers fade.

Care needs: Full sun to light shade, tolerates a wide range of soil conditions including North Florida's sandy soils. Deadheading extends the bloom period; allowing seed heads to stand through winter feeds birds and enables self-seeding for the following spring. Divide clumps every 2–3 years.

Building a beautiful Jacksonville landscape is really about plant selection — choosing species that are inherently suited to this climate rather than fighting against it.
