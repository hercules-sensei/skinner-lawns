A Guide to Seeding Your Florida Property This Spring

Spring seeding in North Florida operates on a different calendar than the rest of the country. Jacksonville's transition from winter dormancy to summer heat happens fast, and there's a narrow window where soil temperatures, moisture, and day length align for reliable germination. Miss it, and you're reseeding through the heat of July.

Most lawn care advice you'll find online is written for the Midwest or Mid-Atlantic — regions where spring seeding begins in April or May when the ground finally thaws. In Jacksonville and throughout Northeast Florida, the seeding calendar is completely different. Our soils don't freeze. Our "spring" warm-up starts in February. And our summer heat can arrive aggressively enough by June to stress newly germinated seedlings before they've had time to develop deep roots. Timing is everything.

Understanding Florida's Seeding Window

Warm-season grasses — including Bahia, Bermuda, and Centipede — need soil temperatures consistently above 65°F for reliable germination, with 70°F being the more dependable target. In Jacksonville, soil temperatures at the 4-inch depth typically cross the 65°F threshold in late February to early March, reaching 70°F by late March or early April depending on weather patterns for the year.

The ideal seeding window for North Florida is mid-March through late April. During this period, soil temperatures are warm enough for germination, daytime highs are comfortable for seedling establishment (typically 75–85°F), and Jacksonville's spring rainfall patterns provide natural irrigation supplementation. By the time the summer heat arrives in force, properly seeded lawns have 60–90 days of establishment under their roots.

Timing Tip: A cheap soil thermometer from any garden center is worth the investment. Take readings at the 4-inch depth in the morning for three consecutive days — when you're consistently seeing 68°F or above, conditions are right for seeding Bahia or Bermuda.

Grass Types Available from Seed in North Florida

An important clarification upfront: the most common residential grasses in Jacksonville — St. Augustine and Zoysia — are not reliably available as seed. They must be installed as sod or plugs. If you're seeding a large area in North Florida, your main options are:

  • Bahia Grass (Pensacola variety): The most practical seeded grass for large North Florida properties. Pensacola Bahia is cold-hardy, drought-tolerant once established, and widely available. Seeding rate is approximately 7–10 lbs per 1,000 square feet of pure seed.
  • Bermudagrass (Common or Hulled varieties): Fast-germinating and aggressive once established. Better suited to athletic fields and open commercial areas than fine residential lawns due to its invasive spreading habit. Seeding rate is 1–2 lbs per 1,000 square feet of hulled seed.
  • Centipede Grass: Can be seeded but germinates very slowly (3–4 weeks) and is sensitive to soil pH. Best for North Florida properties in full sun with acidic soil below pH 6.0. Use approximately 0.25–0.5 lbs per 1,000 square feet — it's tiny seed, so less is more.

Soil Preparation: The Step Most Homeowners Skip

Jacksonville sits on some of the sandiest soils in North Florida. Duval County soils are predominantly Entisols — fine sands with very low organic matter content and poor water retention. Seeding into unprepared sand is a recipe for failure. Before spreading a single seed, your soil needs attention.

Step 1: Soil Test

The University of Florida IFAS Extension in Duval County offers affordable soil testing. A basic test tells you pH, organic matter level, and major nutrient status. Most Jacksonville soils test below pH 6.0 and benefit from lime application 2–4 weeks before seeding. Don't guess on pH — it affects nutrient availability more than almost any other factor.

Step 2: Scalp and Remove Existing Debris

Mow any existing vegetation as low as your mower will go. Rake out dead thatch and debris. Seed needs direct soil contact to germinate — thatch acts as a physical barrier between seed and soil.

Step 3: Loosen the Top 1–2 Inches

Use a garden rake or a lawn aerator to break up the soil surface. On larger areas, a slit-seeder (available at local equipment rental yards on Normandy Boulevard and the Southside) places seed directly into small slits in the soil, dramatically improving germination rates compared to broadcast seeding.

Step 4: Amend Organic Matter

Incorporate a thin layer of compost (about ¼ inch) into the top inch of soil. This improves moisture retention in Jacksonville's fast-draining sandy soils and gives germinating seedlings a better nutrient environment during their first weeks.

Seeding Technique and Coverage

Broadcast seeding with a drop spreader or hand-crank spreader is effective for small areas. For yards larger than a quarter-acre, a slit-seeder significantly outperforms surface broadcasting in North Florida's conditions. After seeding, lightly rake the surface to ensure seed-to-soil contact without burying the seed more than ¼ inch deep — most small grass seeds need light to germinate and should sit near the surface.

Apply a light layer of straw mulch (not hay — hay carries weed seeds) or a paper-based erosion control blanket on slopes. Jacksonville's afternoon thunderstorms can wash bare seeded areas dramatically on any slope greater than 5%.

Watering Your Newly Seeded Lawn

Germinating grass seed must stay consistently moist without being waterlogged. In Jacksonville's spring heat, this typically means light irrigation twice daily — a 5–10 minute run in early morning and a brief afternoon refresh around 2–3 PM — until seedlings reach about 1 inch in height. Once established, begin transitioning to deeper, less frequent watering to encourage root development.

Be aware of St. Johns River Water Management District (SJRWMD) watering restrictions. New lawn establishment is exempt from the standard 2-days-per-week restriction for up to 60 days from installation, but you must apply for this exemption. Your irrigation installer or landscaping contractor can help you document the new installation date.

Winter Overseeding with Ryegrass

One of the most popular lawn practices in Jacksonville — and one that confuses homeowners new to the area — is overseeding warm-season lawns with annual ryegrass in October or November. When your St. Augustine or Bahia goes dormant and turns tan, an overseeding of annual ryegrass (applied at about 5–10 lbs per 1,000 sq ft) keeps the lawn looking green through the winter months. This is purely aesthetic — the ryegrass dies naturally as temperatures rise in spring, allowing your warm-season turf to green back up underneath. It's a seasonal visual solution, not a permanent fix for lawn thin spots.

Whether you're establishing a new lawn from seed or overseeding for winter color, timing and soil preparation are the two variables that most determine success.
