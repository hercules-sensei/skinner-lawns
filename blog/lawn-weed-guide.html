Lawn Weed Guide: Identifying Common Weeds in Your Jacksonville Lawn

Jacksonville's warm, humid climate and sandy Duval County soils create near-perfect conditions for aggressive lawn weeds year-round. Knowing what you're dealing with is the first step toward a thick, healthy turf — so here's a practical guide to the most common culprits we see on Northeast Florida properties.

Weeds don't take a season off in North Florida. While homeowners in other parts of the country get a winter reprieve, Jacksonville's mild winters allow many broadleaf and grassy weeds to keep germinating and spreading from October through March. By the time St. Augustine or Zoysia grass greens back up in spring, weeds can already have a substantial foothold. Identification is critical — the wrong herbicide applied to the wrong weed (or at the wrong time) wastes money and can injure desirable turf.

Dallisgrass (Paspalum dilatatum)

What It Looks Like

Dallisgrass is a coarse, clumping perennial grass that grows in circular patches. Its blades are wide and rough-textured with a distinctive membranous ligule at the base of each leaf. The seed stalks shoot up quickly after mowing, producing finger-like seed spikes that are hard to miss. The clumps grow faster than surrounding turf, giving the lawn an uneven, lumpy texture.

Why It Spreads in Florida

Dallisgrass thrives in moist, low-lying areas and tolerates the saturated soils that are common in Duval and St. Johns County after summer rain events. It spreads primarily through seed and through underground rhizomes, which makes hand-pulling ineffective — any root fragment left behind will regrow. Jacksonville's long growing season gives it months to establish before any dormancy slows it down.

How to Control It

Selective post-emergent herbicides containing MSMA (where still registered) or halosulfuron-methyl can suppress Dallisgrass in St. Augustine lawns, but multiple applications are typically needed. Because Dallisgrass is a perennial with deep rhizomes, professional treatment from a certified applicator is far more effective than consumer spot treatments. Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale)

What It Looks Like

Most Jacksonville homeowners recognize the dandelion's bright yellow flower and the familiar puffball seed head. The leaves form a basal rosette with deeply toothed margins. The thick taproot can reach several inches into Florida's sandy soils, making hand removal difficult unless done while the plant is small and the soil is moist.

Why It Spreads in Florida

Dandelions prefer the cooler months, germinating prolifically in fall and persisting through Jacksonville's mild winters. Each mature plant can produce up to 200 seeds per flower head, and those seeds drift easily in Florida's coastal breezes. Thin or stressed turf — common in shaded areas under live oaks — gives dandelion seeds exactly the bare soil they need to establish.

How to Control It

Broadleaf herbicides containing 2,4-D, dicamba, or triclopyr are effective when applied in late fall before seeds set. A healthy, dense lawn is your best long-term defense: proper fertilization timing, correct mowing height, and adequate irrigation close the canopy gaps that dandelions exploit.

Creeping Beggarweed (Desmodium incanum)

What It Looks Like

Creeping Beggarweed is a low-growing perennial legume with small oval leaflets arranged in groups of three, similar in appearance to clover. Its tiny pink-purple flowers bloom throughout the warm season, and the seed pods are notorious for clinging to clothing, pet fur, and lawn equipment — one of its primary dispersal mechanisms.

Why It Spreads in Florida

As a nitrogen-fixing legume, Creeping Beggarweed actually thrives in low-fertility soils where competing grasses are weak. North Florida's sandy, nutrient-poor soils in areas of Duval and Clay County are exactly the environment where this weed gains the upper hand. It spreads aggressively through its sticky seeds and can rapidly colonize bare patches left by drought, pest damage, or improper mowing.

How to Control It

Post-emergent broadleaf herbicides are effective but must be applied during active growth. Improving overall lawn density through proper nutrition and irrigation is equally important — a thick St. Augustine or Zoysia lawn naturally suppresses Beggarweed germination.

Broadleaf Plantain (Plantago major)

What It Looks Like

Broadleaf Plantain has wide, oval leaves with prominent parallel veins and a smooth texture. It grows in a flat rosette directly from a fibrous root system. A tall, narrow seed spike emerges from the center of the rosette. Unlike many weeds, plantain tolerates compacted soils and heavy foot traffic, which is why it frequently appears in Jacksonville lawns along driveways, paths, and high-traffic play areas.

Why It Spreads in Florida

Florida's frequent rainfall keeps soils moist, accelerating Broadleaf Plantain germination from spring through fall. It produces enormous quantities of seed and is highly adaptable to the range of soil conditions found across Northeast Florida — from the well-drained sandy soils of St. Johns County to the heavier clay soils in parts of Nassau County.

How to Control It

Standard broadleaf herbicide formulations work well on Plantain during active growth. Aerating compacted areas and overseeding thin patches removes the establishment advantage this weed relies upon.

Quackgrass (Elymus repens)

What It Looks Like

Quackgrass is a coarse perennial grass with wide, rough blades and distinctive claw-like auricles that clasp the stem at the leaf sheath. It spreads aggressively through white, wiry underground rhizomes that can extend several feet from the parent plant. In a fine-textured St. Augustine lawn, Quackgrass patches are clearly visible due to their lighter color and coarser texture.

Why It Spreads in Florida

Quackgrass is more common in North Florida than in Central or South Florida, as it prefers slightly cooler temperatures during establishment — Jacksonville's winter months fit that window well. Once established, the deep rhizome network makes it nearly impossible to eradicate without targeted herbicide programs.

How to Control It

Non-selective herbicides like glyphosate will kill Quackgrass but also injure surrounding turf. In most residential situations, a professional applicator will use a selective approach or spot-treat to minimize turf damage. Repeated applications are typically required to exhaust the rhizome system.

Yellow Woodsorrel (Oxalis stricta)

What It Looks Like

Yellow Woodsorrel is easy to recognize by its heart-shaped leaflets in groups of three, which closely resemble clover but have a distinctive sour taste if chewed (though we don't recommend it). Bright yellow five-petaled flowers appear throughout the growing season. The slender seed pods explode when ripe, launching seeds several feet in all directions — a highly effective dispersal strategy.

Why It Spreads in Florida

Yellow Woodsorrel is a year-round problem in Jacksonville. It germinates in cool weather, persists through summer in shaded areas, and re-establishes quickly after any lawn disturbance. It is prolific in the partial shade under Florida's ubiquitous live oak canopy, where turf grasses are already under stress from low light.

How to Control It

Post-emergent broadleaf herbicides containing triclopyr provide good control. Because Woodsorrel re-seeds so aggressively, timing applications before seed pods form is important. A pre-emergent herbicide program in early spring can dramatically reduce the seed bank in heavily infested lawns.

The Case for Professional Weed Control

Consumer herbicide products at big-box stores are often formulated for broad tolerances and general use. In Jacksonville's specific climate — intense summer heat, occasional cold snaps, sandy soils with variable pH, and the predominance of St. Augustine grass — getting herbicide selection, timing, and application rate right matters enormously. An incorrect application can cause turfgrass phytotoxicity just as your lawn is trying to green up.
