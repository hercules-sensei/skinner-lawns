Top 10 Native Plants to Grow in Northeast Florida

Native plants are the smartest investment you can make in a Jacksonville landscape. Adapted over thousands of years to North Florida's sandy soils, humid summers, and occasional winter freezes, they require less water, less fertilizer, and less intervention than most ornamental imports — while providing irreplaceable habitat for the region's native wildlife.

The case for native plants in Northeast Florida goes beyond aesthetics. The St. Johns River watershed is one of the most ecologically significant in the Southeast, and the landscaping choices made by homeowners across Duval, St. Johns, Clay, Nassau, and Flagler counties collectively influence water quality, wildlife corridors, and the long-term health of that ecosystem. Native plants — adapted to local rainfall patterns, soil chemistry, and seasonal cycles — do this naturally, without chemical inputs, simply by existing in the landscape.

The ten plants below are all native to Northeast Florida and all perform beautifully in residential and commercial landscape settings. Many are available at the Jacksonville Arboretum plant sales, Florida Native Plant Society chapters, and an increasing number of local nurseries throughout the region.

1. Firebush (Hamelia patens)

Firebush is one of the most reliably beautiful and wildlife-supportive shrubs available to Northeast Florida gardeners. From late spring until the first frost, it produces clusters of tubular orange-red flowers that are irresistible to hummingbirds and several species of native butterflies, including the Zebra Longwing (Florida's state butterfly). The flowers give way to clusters of deep purple-red berries that birds devour throughout fall and winter. It grows vigorously to 5–10 feet in Jacksonville's climate and tolerates light shade under pine canopy remarkably well for a sun-loving plant.

2. American Beautyberry (Callicarpa americana)

Beautyberry earns its name in September and October when its arching branches become studded with clusters of intensely vivid magenta-purple berries — one of the most electrifying sights in any North Florida garden. The berries are beloved by mockingbirds, robins, and dozens of other species. The plant itself grows as a loose, arching shrub to about 6 feet and is completely at home in the partial shade at the edge of a woodland garden or along a fence line. It dies back after frost but re-sprouts vigorously in spring, making it essentially maintenance-free in Jacksonville's climate.

3. Gulf Muhly Grass (Muhlenbergia capillaris)

Gulf Muhly's October bloom — a cloud of gossamer pink-purple flower plumes above fine green foliage — is one of the most dramatic seasonal displays in Northeast Florida. This compact, clumping grass thrives in Jacksonville's sandy soils and full sun with virtually no supplemental irrigation once established. Mass plantings of five or more plants create a visual effect that stops traffic during peak bloom. Cut back to 6 inches in late winter; otherwise, leave it alone. Few plants deliver more beauty per unit of maintenance.

4. Sabal Palm (Sabal palmetto)

Florida's state tree is irreplaceable as a structural element in Northeast Florida landscapes. Cold-hardy to the low teens (Fahrenheit), wind-resistant, salt-spray tolerant, and adaptable to a remarkable range of soils and moisture conditions, the Sabal Palm is genuinely one of the toughest landscape trees available in this region. Its costapalmate fronds create dramatic shadow play in the afternoon sun, and its trunk — which develops the characteristic "bootjack" pattern of old leaf bases over time — adds textural interest that no other tree can replicate.

5. Yaupon Holly (Ilex vomitoria)

Yaupon Holly may have the most unfortunate botanical name in the plant kingdom, but it is one of the most useful and versatile native shrubs for Jacksonville landscapes. It grows naturally across Northeast Florida in coastal scrubs, flatwoods edges, and disturbed areas — a testament to its toughness. In the landscape, it can be pruned into a small multi-trunk tree, shaped as a formal hedge, or allowed to grow into a naturalistic shrub mass. Female plants produce small, bright red berries in winter that persist for months and feed birds through the cold season. It tolerates full sun to heavy shade, wet or dry soil, and occasional saltwater flooding — essentially no other shrub is this adaptable in North Florida conditions.

6. Gaillardia / Blanket Flower (Gaillardia pulchella)

Blanket Flower is the most cheerful wildflower in the Northeast Florida palette. Its bold flowers — typically red or orange with yellow tips — bloom from spring through fall, attracting native bees and butterflies throughout the growing season. It thrives in Jacksonville's sandy, well-drained soils and full sun, and actually performs better in low-fertility conditions than in enriched garden beds. Use it in naturalized plantings, sunny borders, or coastal gardens where salt spray challenges other plants. It self-seeds freely, creating a naturalistic colony over time.

7. Coreopsis (Coreopsis lanceolata and C. floridana)

Coreopsis is Florida's official state wildflower, and both lanceolated and Florida Coreopsis are native to Northeast Florida. The bright golden-yellow daisy flowers appear in late spring and again in fall, with sporadic blooms throughout summer in Jacksonville's climate. Like Gaillardia, it thrives in poor, sandy soils with good drainage and is essentially self-maintaining in naturalized settings. It's a cornerstone plant for pollinator gardens and wildflower meadow plantings across Duval and St. Johns County.

8. Wild Azalea / Florida Azalea (Rhododendron austrinum)

While most ornamental azaleas sold at Jacksonville garden centers are non-native Asian introductions, the Florida Azalea is a native species native to the Panhandle and spreading into North Florida that delivers comparable floral impact with genuine ecological value. It produces masses of golden-yellow to orange flowers in late winter to early spring — often before the leaves fully emerge — creating a spectacular display at the end of the dormant season. It prefers the partial shade of mature pine or oak woodland, making it ideal for the shaded areas of Jacksonville properties where many flowering plants struggle.

9. Swamp Milkweed (Asclepias incarnata)

No garden in Northeast Florida that claims to support pollinators is complete without milkweed. Swamp Milkweed is the species best suited to Jacksonville's frequently moist, low-lying soils. It produces clusters of pink to mauve flowers from late spring through summer, and its foliage is the sole food source for Monarch butterfly caterpillars. With Monarch populations under significant pressure from habitat loss, planting native milkweed is a genuinely meaningful act of conservation. Swamp Milkweed reaches 3–4 feet and works well in rain garden plantings, moist areas near drainage swales, and any spot in the yard that tends to hold water.

10. Saw Palmetto (Serenoa repens)

Saw Palmetto is the defining understory plant of Northeast Florida's natural landscapes — the low-growing palm with fan-shaped fronds and serrated stems that carpets the ground beneath longleaf pine and live oak canopy across Duval and St. Johns County. In a landscape setting, it provides unmatched structural texture as a groundcover for large, sunny or partially shaded areas. It requires essentially zero maintenance once established, tolerates drought, salt spray, poor soils, and periodic flooding. Its cream-colored spring flower spikes produce berries that are critically important to black bears, deer, and dozens of bird species. Few plants better capture the wild spirit of Northeast Florida's native landscape.

Why Plant Natives?

Beyond individual plant performance, there's a systems-level benefit to native plantings that conventional ornamentals can't provide. Native plants and native wildlife evolved together — the berries, seeds, nectar, and foliage of Northeast Florida's native species are specifically adapted to feed the insects, birds, and mammals of this region. A landscape built around native plants genuinely functions as habitat, not just decoration. And from a practical standpoint, a well-designed native planting bed requires a fraction of the irrigation, fertilizer, and pesticide input of conventional ornamental beds once established — a meaningful savings on both time and cost over a five-to-ten year horizon.
