Irrigation System Tips: Preparing for Florida's Growing Season

Spring in Northeast Florida is a short window — soil temperatures rise fast, warm-season grasses break dormancy quickly, and the first true irrigation demand of the year arrives before many homeowners have thought to check their system. A pre-season irrigation inspection catches problems before they cost you a summer of poor lawn performance.

Jacksonville's dry season peaks in spring — April and May are consistently the two driest months in Duval County, and that timing aligns almost perfectly with the period when warm-season turf is making its transition out of dormancy and establishing the deep root system that will carry it through summer. An irrigation system that's leaking, has clogged heads, or has a misconfigured controller during this critical window can mean patchy green-up, drought stress, and weed establishment in the bare spots. Don't wait until June to discover a problem that was there in February.

Pre-Season Inspection Checklist

Walk through this checklist each February or early March before you increase irrigation for the growing season. It takes about 45 minutes for an average Jacksonville residential property, and it prevents problems that can cost hundreds or thousands of dollars to address mid-season.

Controller and Programming

  • Check the date, time, and day settings. After winter power interruptions or battery replacements, controllers often reset to incorrect dates. A controller running on the wrong day schedule means you may be irrigating on restricted days without realizing it — a SJRWMD violation.
  • Update seasonal run times. If you reduced run times for winter, now is the time to bring them back up for spring. Most Jacksonville lawns need ¾ inch per zone per event during the spring dry season — calculate your run times using the catch-cup method described in our irrigation watering guide.
  • Confirm watering days match your SJRWMD schedule. Even-numbered addresses water on Thursdays and Sundays; odd-numbered addresses water on Wednesdays and Saturdays. Confirm your controller is programmed accordingly.
  • Check for "skip day" settings that may have been set for winter. Many smart controllers and even standard timers have a skip or interval feature that may have been set to run every 7 days for winter — confirm it's been returned to the proper twice-weekly schedule.

Rain Sensor Inspection

  • Locate and inspect the sensor. Rain sensors on Jacksonville irrigation systems are most commonly mounted on the roofline, fence, or irrigation valve box cover. They're often forgotten entirely until they fail to prevent irrigation during a downpour.
  • Test sensor operation. Activate a zone manually, then trigger the sensor with water from a hose or by pressing the test mechanism. The controller should stop the zone within 30–60 seconds. If it doesn't, the sensor is either defective, disconnected, or needs replacement.
  • Clean the sensor disk. Most rain sensors use absorbent hygroscopic disks that expand when wet to break the irrigation circuit. Over time, these disks can dry out completely and crack, or become clogged with algae and debris, preventing proper operation. Many can be cleaned; others need replacement (they typically cost $15–35 at local irrigation supply houses).
  • Verify the bypass switch is in the "active" position. Most rain sensor installations include a manual bypass switch on the controller, which if set to "bypass" effectively disables the sensor entirely. This is a common discovery on service calls.

Zone-by-Zone Head Inspection

Run each zone for 3–5 minutes and visually inspect every head. Walk the zone while it's running — this is the only reliable way to catch problems. Look for:

  • Heads not popping up: Often caused by a clogged filter screen, damaged pop-up mechanism, or low pressure. Sandy soils in Northeast Florida are notorious for working their way into head filter screens over time — these unscrew easily for cleaning.
  • Heads spraying in the wrong direction: Rotor heads can be rotated off their original arc setting over time by foot traffic, mowing equipment, or soil movement. Most rotors have an adjustment slot accessible with a small flathead screwdriver while the head is operating.
  • Heads with damaged nozzles: Mower damage is the number-one cause of head replacement in residential Jacksonville systems. Look for cracked caps, missing nozzles, and heads that spray in 360° when they should be a partial arc — all common signs of impact damage.
  • Heads sunken below grade: Turf growth over winter can push soil and thatch up around head bodies, reducing pop-up height and causing irrigation distribution problems. These can often be raised by adjusting the riser body, or may need to be cut and reset.
  • Leaking heads: A head that continues spraying for 1–2 minutes after the zone shuts off has a failed check valve — this causes low-head drainage that puddles water around the lowest head in the zone after every irrigation cycle. Replacement valve check kits are inexpensive and straightforward for the DIYer.

Visual Leak Detection

  • Check water meter for movement when all water is off. With every water-using device in the house turned off (no toilets running, no faucets, no appliances), check your water meter. If the low-flow indicator (a small triangle or dial) is moving, you have a leak somewhere — possibly in the irrigation mainline.
  • Look for unexplained wet spots in the lawn. Persistent wet areas in the lawn away from irrigation heads during dry periods indicate an underground pipe leak. In Jacksonville's sandy soils, pipe breaks can erode significant soil cavities before the surface becomes wet — don't ignore them.
  • Inspect valve boxes. Open all valve boxes and check for water accumulation, damaged wires, and corrosion on valve solenoids. Standing water in valve boxes can indicate a minor mainline leak draining into the box.

Zone-by-Zone Watering Needs: Not All Zones Are Equal

One of the most common irrigation mistakes in Jacksonville residential systems is running all zones on the same schedule. In reality, different zones have dramatically different water requirements:

  • Sunny lawn zones: Highest demand. Run at full frequency (twice weekly) and full run time during spring and summer. These zones typically need 30–50 minutes depending on head type to deliver ¾ inch.
  • Shaded lawn zones: Under Jacksonville's mature live oak and pine canopy, turf receives reduced sunlight and has lower evapotranspiration rates. These zones often need 25–35% less irrigation time than comparable sunny zones. Overwatering shaded St. Augustine is a major contributor to fungal disease in North Florida.
  • Drip irrigation zones (planting beds): If you have drip irrigation in landscape beds, run times and frequencies differ entirely from spray and rotor zones. Native and Florida-adapted plants typically need far less water than turf zones — many established native plant beds in Jacksonville need supplemental irrigation only during extended dry spells.
  • Slope and drainage considerations: Any zone that irrigates a slope should be programmed with shorter, more frequent cycles (called "cycle and soak") to prevent runoff. In the sandy soils of St. Johns County hillsides and elevated neighborhoods like Mandarin, water can run off slopes before it has time to penetrate the surface.

When to Call a Professional vs. DIY

Many irrigation maintenance tasks are genuinely DIY-friendly — head cleaning, nozzle replacement, controller reprogramming, and minor head adjustments are within the ability of most handy homeowners. However, some situations call for a licensed irrigation contractor:

  • Underground pipe breaks or mainline leaks: Diagnosing and repairing pressurized mainline leaks requires proper equipment and typically involves excavation — not a DIY project for most homeowners.
  • Valve failure: Irrigation valves that won't open or close properly, or that leak through the diaphragm, need to be replaced. While not technically difficult, improper valve replacement can introduce system pressure problems.
  • System re-design or zone additions: Adding zones, moving heads, or redesigning coverage to accommodate new landscaping requires hydraulic calculations to ensure adequate pressure is maintained across all zones — a professional irrigation contractor will size components correctly.
  • Backflow preventer testing and certification: SJRWMD and Jacksonville municipal utility requirements mandate annual testing of backflow prevention assemblies on most commercial systems and some residential systems. This must be performed by a licensed tester and reported to the utility.
