How to Care for Your Lawn During Florida's Winter Months

Jacksonville's winters are nothing like South Florida's — we get genuine cold fronts, occasional freezes, and temperatures that can genuinely stress warm-season turf. What you do (and don't do) from November through February has a significant impact on how well your lawn rebounds when spring arrives.

If you've lived in Jacksonville for a few years, you know that telling out-of-state friends "it freezes here" often gets some skepticism. But North Florida sits in a different climate zone than Orlando and Miami. Duval County regularly sees overnight lows in the 30s from December through February, and hard freezes — nights below 28°F for four or more hours — occur a few times each decade. That's enough to tip warm-season grasses into full dormancy and, in cold snaps, cause real damage. Understanding what's happening underground and what actions genuinely help versus what wastes your time is the foundation of good winter lawn care.

What Actually Happens to Florida Lawns in Winter

Dormancy vs. Damage

All of Jacksonville's common warm-season grasses — St. Augustine, Zoysia, Centipede, Bahia — enter dormancy when soil temperatures consistently drop below 55°F. Dormancy is not death. The plant is alive underground, conserving energy in its root system. The blades turn tan to gray-brown, growth stops, and the lawn essentially hibernates until soil temperatures climb back above 60°F in late February or March.

Actual cold damage — injury to the crowns or root system — is a different matter. St. Augustine and Centipede are the most cold-sensitive common Jacksonville grasses. A hard freeze following a period of unseasonably warm weather (which encourages new tender growth) poses the greatest risk, as soft, actively growing tissue is far less cold-hardy than properly hardened-off dormant turf.

The North Florida Difference

Lawn care advice aimed at Central or South Florida doesn't fully apply here. Orlando rarely sees freezes; South Florida almost never does. In Jacksonville and St. Johns County, we're in USDA Zone 9a, and our landscape management must account for that reality. A Palmetto St. Augustine lawn that looks perfect in December in Fort Lauderdale would look very different after a hard freeze in Mandarin or Ponte Vedra Beach.

Fertilizer Timing: When to Stop and When to Restart

This is one of the most important — and most frequently mismanaged — aspects of winter lawn care in Jacksonville. The rule is straightforward: stop applying nitrogen-containing fertilizers by October 1, and don't apply again until late March or when the lawn shows clear signs of active growth resumption.

Applying nitrogen fertilizer in November or December forces new, soft growth that is highly susceptible to freeze damage. That lush green flush you might see after a fall application looks great right up until the first hard freeze burns it completely. The Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (FDACS) restricts fertilizer application during the "blackout period" in many Florida counties specifically for this reason — though Jacksonville's ordinances allow fall applications with appropriate restrictions on phosphorus and nitrogen rates.

Your last fertilizer application of the season should ideally be a potassium-heavy formula in September or early October. Potassium strengthens cell walls and improves cold hardiness — think of it as toughening up the grass before winter. This is a step many homeowners skip and professionals don't.

Reducing Irrigation Through Winter

Dormant grass has dramatically reduced water needs compared to actively growing turf. Continuing a summer irrigation schedule through winter wastes water, invites fungal disease, and can actually keep the turf from properly hardening off for cold weather. Under the St. Johns River Water Management District (SJRWMD) rules, most Northeast Florida addresses are restricted to once-per-week irrigation from November 1 through March 31.

In practice, many Jacksonville lawns need even less than once per week during cool, rainy winter months. Adjust your irrigation controller for seasonal conditions: if you're getting regular rainfall, turn the system off and let it rain. During dry stretches — North Florida can go two to three weeks without rain in winter — a single deep irrigation cycle is sufficient to prevent desiccation of the dormant root system.

Smart Controller Tip: A rain sensor or smart irrigation controller pays for itself in a single Florida winter by automatically suspending irrigation after rainfall. SJRWMD requires rain sensors on all irrigation systems installed after May 1991 — if yours doesn't have one, it's both a regulatory compliance issue and a water waste issue.

Winter Mowing: Slow Down and Raise the Deck

Mowing frequency drops significantly when grass is dormant — a St. Augustine lawn that needed weekly mowing in July might go three to four weeks between cuts in January. When you do mow, raise the mowing deck slightly: cutting dormant St. Augustine below 3 inches removes leaf tissue that insulates the crown from cold. The thatch and leaf tissue above the crown provides meaningful protection when overnight temperatures dip into the 30s.

Don't mow when frost is on the ground. Walking on frost-covered turf — especially with mowing equipment — physically damages the frozen leaf cells. Wait until midday when temperatures have risen above freezing and any frost has melted before mowing or doing any significant lawn work.

Overseeding with Annual Ryegrass for Winter Color

One of Jacksonville's most popular winter lawn practices is overseeding dormant warm-season turf with annual ryegrass in October or November. The ryegrass — applied at 5–10 pounds per 1,000 square feet — germinates quickly in fall temperatures, turning a tan dormant lawn into a bright green winter carpet.

Annual ryegrass is a cool-season grass that dies naturally as temperatures rise above 85°F in late spring, at which point the warm-season grass underneath resumes active growth. The transition usually happens smoothly in Jacksonville by late April or May. This is strictly a cosmetic practice — it doesn't improve the underlying warm-season turf — but it's popular in residential neighborhoods and is essentially standard practice for commercial properties and HOA common areas where appearance year-round is a priority.

If you overseed, scale back fertilizer applications on the ryegrass — it's already a fast-growing, vigorous grass that doesn't need much encouragement in Jacksonville's winter conditions. A light application of balanced fertilizer in December is sufficient to keep it lush without creating a maintenance burden.

Preparing for Spring Green-Up

The work you do in late February sets the tone for your entire growing season. As soil temperatures begin climbing toward 60°F, you can take several steps to support a strong green-up:

  • Core aeration: Aerating just before the growing season begins loosens compacted soils and improves oxygen and water penetration to the root zone. This is the single best time of year to aerate in North Florida.
  • Soil test and pH adjustment: If you haven't tested recently, do it now. Lime applications made in February or March have time to work before the main growing season begins.
  • Pre-emergent herbicide: Apply a pre-emergent between February 15 and March 1 in Jacksonville — before soil temperatures reach 65°F — to prevent crabgrass and other summer annual weeds from germinating. Timing this correctly is critical; too early and the product degrades before weeds germinate, too late and the weeds are already past the stage where pre-emergents are effective.
  • First fertilizer application: Wait until you see at least 50% of the lawn actively greening before applying any nitrogen. Applying fertilizer to fully dormant turf accomplishes nothing except feeding weeds and potentially running off into storm drains.

Winter lawn care in Northeast Florida is less about dramatic interventions and more about restraint — pulling back on irrigation, fertilizer, and mowing frequency while positioning the lawn well for a strong spring comeback.
