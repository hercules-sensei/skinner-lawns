The Top 5 Worst Weeds Threatening Your Jacksonville Lawn

Not all lawn weeds are created equal. Some are nuisances you can manage with consistent mowing and a healthy turf. Others are genuine threats — aggressive spreaders that, if left unchecked, can overtake large sections of your Jacksonville lawn within a single growing season. These five are the ones that demand your attention.

Jacksonville's combination of year-round warmth, ample rainfall, sandy soils, and long growing season creates ideal conditions for some of the most persistent weeds in the Southeast. Understanding what makes each of these five particularly aggressive — and what control strategies actually work — is the difference between staying ahead of them and losing ground every summer.

1. Crabgrass (Digitaria sanguinalis and D. ischaemum)

Why It's a Top Threat in Jacksonville

Crabgrass is an annual grass that completes its entire life cycle — germination, growth, seed production, and death — within a single year. The reason it's so threatening is purely mathematical: a single crabgrass plant produces 150,000 seeds before it dies in fall, and those seeds remain viable in the soil for years. In Jacksonville, crabgrass germinates when soil temperatures at the 2-inch depth reach 55–60°F — typically in late February to early March — which means it gets a head start on warm-season grasses still in dormancy.

Identification

Crabgrass has a distinctive low, spreading growth habit with stems that radiate outward from a central point, lying nearly flat to the ground. The blades are wide and coarse compared to most lawn grasses. In St. Augustine or Zoysia lawns, it's clearly visible as lighter-colored, coarser patches that spread in circles. By late summer, the finger-like seed heads protrude above the lawn canopy.

Prevention vs. Treatment

The single most effective weapon against crabgrass is a correctly timed pre-emergent herbicide application. In Jacksonville, this means applying between February 15 and March 1 — before soil temperatures reach 60°F. Products containing pendimethalin or prodiamine are standard choices for residential lawns. Post-emergent crabgrass control is far less effective and must be applied when plants are young (fewer than five leaf blades) — attempting to kill mature crabgrass in July with post-emergents is largely futile.

Dense, healthy turf is your best long-term defense. Crabgrass germinates in bare soil and thin turf; a thick St. Augustine lawn at the right mowing height (3.5–4 inches) significantly suppresses crabgrass germination by shading the soil surface.

2. Virginia Buttonweed (Diodia virginiana)

Why It's a Top Threat in Jacksonville

Virginia Buttonweed has a special distinction in Northeast Florida lawn care circles: it is consistently ranked by University of Florida IFAS turfgrass specialists as the most difficult weed to control in Florida lawns. That reputation is well-earned. It spreads through three separate mechanisms simultaneously — seeds, stems that root at every node they contact, and an underground stem system that regrows from any fragment left in the soil. It thrives specifically in wet, compacted soils, and Jacksonville's summer rainy season creates ideal conditions for explosive growth.

Identification

Virginia Buttonweed has opposite, dark-green glossy leaves and produces small, white, four-petaled flowers at the leaf axils throughout summer. It grows prostrate to the ground, spreading outward in dense mats that smother surrounding turf. In heavy infestations, it can completely replace the desired turfgrass over large areas. Look for it especially in low spots, near downspouts, and in areas with chronically wet soils.

Prevention vs. Treatment

Improving drainage in affected areas is step one — Virginia Buttonweed simply cannot compete in well-drained, properly maintained turf. Chemical control typically requires repeated applications of herbicides containing metsulfuron-methyl or carfentrazone throughout the growing season. Complete eradication in a heavily infested lawn often takes two or more full growing seasons even with professional treatment. There are no shortcuts with this weed.

3. Thistle (Cirsium spp.)

Why It's a Top Threat in Jacksonville

Thistles present two distinct problems: their deep taproots make physical removal difficult, and their feathery seeds travel remarkable distances on the breeze — a single mature thistle plant can produce up to 4,000 seeds that drift hundreds of feet from the parent plant. Several thistle species are found in Northeast Florida, with Spiny Sowthistle and Bull Thistle being among the most common in Duval and Clay County landscapes.

Identification

Thistles are hard to miss: deeply lobed leaves with sharp spines along the margins, and in mature plants, purple to pink thistle-flower heads atop tall stalks. Young plants form a flat rosette close to the ground before the central stalk elongates — this juvenile stage is when they're both easier to identify accurately and more responsive to herbicide treatment.

Prevention vs. Treatment

Thistles are biennial or perennial in most Florida conditions, making them persistent even with treatment. Broadleaf herbicides containing 2,4-D or dicamba are effective when applied during the active rosette stage. Physical removal works on young plants if you get the entire taproot — but wear thick gloves. Mowing before seed heads form prevents reproduction but doesn't kill the plant. In established infestations, professional treatment typically achieves better results than repeated consumer product applications.

4. Nutsedge (Cyperus esculentus and C. rotundus)

Why It's a Top Threat in Jacksonville

Nutsedge — commonly called "nutgrass" — is technically a sedge, not a grass, and this distinction matters enormously for control: most grass-selective herbicides don't affect it at all. Yellow Nutsedge (C. esculentus) and Purple Nutsedge (C. rotundus) are both highly problematic in Northeast Florida lawns. Nutsedge reproduces through underground tubers (the "nuts") that can remain viable in the soil for years. Pulling nutsedge by hand actually stimulates the formation of additional tubers — each plant you pull can result in several new plants sprouting nearby.

Identification

Nutsedge grows faster than surrounding turf and consistently stands above the lawn canopy even after mowing — that telltale taller, lighter-green growth is the first sign of an infestation. The stem is triangular in cross-section (a key identifying feature — most grasses have round stems). Seed heads are yellow-brown or purple depending on species. It proliferates in moist soils, making Jacksonville's wet summer months its prime growth period.

Prevention vs. Treatment

Halosulfuron-methyl (Sedgehammer) is the most effective labeled herbicide for nutsedge control in residential St. Augustine lawns. Sulfentrazone is another option that provides both nutsedge and some broadleaf weed control. Multiple applications are typically required to deplete the tuber bank. Improving soil drainage in affected areas reduces future pressure — nutsedge will not thrive in consistently well-drained soil.

5. Dollar Weed / Dollarweed (Hydrocotyle umbellata)

Why It's a Top Threat in Jacksonville

Dollar Weed is the telltale sign of an over-irrigated or poorly drained Jacksonville lawn. It loves saturated soil conditions and spreads through seeds, rhizomes, and tubers simultaneously — a triple-threat dispersal strategy that makes it very difficult to eradicate once established. In wet summers, it can visibly spread week over week in susceptible areas of the lawn, particularly in low spots and near irrigation heads.

Identification

Dollar Weed takes its name from its round, silver-dollar-sized leaves, which are bright, waxy green with a shallowly scalloped margin and a long petiole that attaches to the center of the leaf (like an umbrella). It grows low and spreads outward in dense mats. In a St. Augustine lawn, it looks similar to the turf at a glance, but the round leaf shape and waxy texture distinguish it clearly on close inspection.

Prevention vs. Treatment

The most effective intervention is reducing soil moisture in affected areas — fix drainage issues, reduce irrigation run times in dollar weed zones, and ensure irrigation heads aren't creating chronically wet spots. On the chemical side, atrazine is registered for dollar weed control in St. Augustine lawns and is a common professional choice. Metsulfuron-methyl provides good post-emergent control as well. As with most lawn weeds, professional treatment achieves more consistent results than repeated consumer product applications, particularly in established infestations.

The Professional Advantage in Weed Control

Consumer herbicide products are broadly formulated and often don't reflect the specific weed pressures, grass types, and seasonal timing of a Jacksonville lawn. A certified professional applicator brings three things that matter: accurate weed identification, the right product for that specific weed in that specific turfgrass, and properly timed applications. Applying a post-emergent when weeds are too mature, or skipping the pre-emergent window, wastes money and delays effective control by an entire season.
