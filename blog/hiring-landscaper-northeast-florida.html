What to Look for When Hiring a Landscaper in Northeast Florida

The Jacksonville landscaping market has a wide range of operators — from properly licensed, insured, and certified professionals to unlicensed individuals working without any of the protections that matter when something goes wrong. Knowing the difference protects your property, your finances, and the people working on your lawn.

A quick search on any local marketplace or neighborhood Facebook group will surface dozens of lawn care and landscaping operators in the Jacksonville area at any given time. Price varies enormously. Quality varies just as much. The challenge is that from the outside, a truck with a trailer looks the same whether it belongs to a licensed, insured professional or an unlicensed operator with no business registration, no insurance, and no training in Florida-specific environmental regulations. Here's how to tell them apart before you hire.

Certifications That Matter in Florida

GI-BMP Certification (Green Industries Best Management Practices)

The GI-BMP certification is administered by the Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (FDACS) and the University of Florida IFAS Extension. Florida law requires that at least one person in each "green industry" business who applies or supervises the application of fertilizer hold a GI-BMP certificate. This training covers Florida-specific fertilizer application rates, timing (including blackout periods), setback requirements from water bodies, and best practices for minimizing nitrogen and phosphorus runoff into the St. Johns River and other Northeast Florida waterways.

Why does this matter to you? Because a landscaper who doesn't understand fertilizer blackout periods, application rates for Florida's sandy soils, or setback requirements near your drainage swale or retention pond is applying products to your property without the knowledge to do it correctly — and any environmental liability associated with runoff can fall back on the property owner in certain circumstances. Ask specifically: "Is your company GI-BMP certified?" A credible professional will have documentation and be able to name the certificate holder.

FDACS Fertilizer Applicator License

Florida Statute 482 requires that any person or business applying fertilizers for compensation hold a current Florida Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services (FDACS) fertilizer license. This is a separate requirement from GI-BMP training. An unlicensed applicator applying fertilizer to your lawn for money is operating illegally in Florida, regardless of how long they've been in business. You can verify FDACS licenses through the department's online license search at freshfromflorida.com — it takes 60 seconds and tells you whether the company you're considering is current and in good standing.

Pesticide Applicator License

If your landscaper offers weed control, pest control, or any herbicide or insecticide applications, Florida law requires them to hold a current Commercial Pesticide Applicator License from FDACS. The specific category relevant to lawn care is Category 4 (Ornamental and Turf Pest Control). An unlicensed person applying herbicides or insecticides to your lawn for compensation is committing a violation of Florida Statute 487. More importantly, an unlicensed applicator hasn't demonstrated knowledge of correct product selection, label compliance, and application rates — which means they may be applying products incorrectly, at the wrong time, or at rates that injure your lawn or violate environmental protection requirements.

Insurance: The Non-Negotiable Baseline

General Liability Insurance

Every landscaping company working on your property should carry general liability insurance at a minimum of $1 million per occurrence. This protects you if they damage your property — a broken window from a rock thrown by a mower, a fence post knocked over by a truck, a sprinkler head smashed by equipment. Without general liability coverage, you're relying entirely on the contractor's willingness to pay for damages out of pocket, which — in practice — often leads to disputes, incomplete repairs, and contractors who simply disappear.

Ask for a Certificate of Insurance (COI) naming you as a certificate holder. Legitimate companies carry these as standard documents and can produce them within 24 hours. A company that hedges, deflects, or provides vague assurances rather than a COI should be disqualified immediately.

Workers' Compensation Insurance

Florida law requires Workers' Compensation coverage for any landscaping business with four or more employees (the threshold is different for some other industries). If one of an uninsured landscaper's employees is injured on your property, Florida courts have in some circumstances found property owners to be secondarily liable for medical costs. This is not a theoretical risk — it happens. Companies with proper workers' comp coverage provide clear COI documentation showing active coverage dates and policy limits.

Even for a solo operator, ask whether they carry a self-employed workers' comp exemption certificate or whether they carry coverage. If an uninsured solo operator is injured on your property, the liability exposure is real.

Equipment Quality: What It Tells You

The equipment a landscaper uses tells you a great deal about how they approach their business — and how they'll treat your property.

Stand-On Mowers vs. Heavy Zero-Turn Riders

This distinction matters significantly for Jacksonville residential properties. Heavy zero-turn riding mowers — the kind with 60"+ decks and 1,000 lbs of weight — create compaction ruts in North Florida's sandy soils, tear up turf on turns, and can leave visible tire tracks in St. Augustine that persist for weeks. Stand-on mowers and lighter-weight commercial walk-behinds distribute weight more evenly and are generally more appropriate for residential turf, particularly in the 10,000–20,000 square foot range typical of Jacksonville neighborhoods.

A landscaper who shows up to your average Mandarin or Nocatee residential lot with a 72" zero-turn appropriate for a golf course or large commercial property isn't necessarily thinking about what's right for your lawn — they're thinking about what's fastest for their schedule. Quality operators match their equipment to the property type.

Blade Sharpness and Equipment Maintenance

Dull mower blades tear rather than cut grass, leaving ragged, frayed blade tips that turn brown and invite disease. You can actually see this on a freshly mowed lawn — a clean cut leaves a smooth, consistent finish; a dull-blade cut leaves tips with a whitish, frayed appearance. Ask how frequently they sharpen blades. Daily sharpening (or at minimum weekly on commercial equipment) is the professional standard. Most consumer operators sharpen blades a few times per year, if that.

Consistency and Communication

In the landscaping industry, the most common complaint — by a wide margin — isn't quality of work. It's reliability and communication. The contractor who did excellent work on the first three visits but has become increasingly inconsistent in showing up, responding to messages, or completing agreed-upon services is unfortunately a common experience in the Jacksonville market.

When evaluating a landscaper, ask specifically: "Will the same crew be servicing my property each visit?" "How do I reach you if there's a problem?" "Do I receive any confirmation when service is completed?" Companies that use service management software can provide visit logs, before-and-after photos, and proactive communication when schedule changes occur. Companies that communicate only by text message to a personal cell phone with sporadic responses are significantly higher risk for service inconsistency.

Red Flags to Watch For

  • No written estimate: Any landscaping work beyond a simple mowing should be documented in writing before work begins. Verbal agreements lead to disputes. If a contractor resists providing a written scope of work and price, treat that as a serious warning sign.
  • No business license or proof of registration: All businesses operating in Jacksonville must hold a current City of Jacksonville or county occupational license. Verify at coj.net or the respective county's tax collector website.
  • Requests for full payment upfront: For project work (sod installation, landscape plantings, irrigation installation), a deposit of 25–50% is reasonable. Full payment before any work is completed is not standard practice and leaves you with no leverage if the work is incomplete or unsatisfactory.
  • No physical address: A company that operates only through a cell number and a social media page, with no verifiable business address or website, is difficult to hold accountable if something goes wrong.
  • Inability to explain their fertilizer and chemical program: A professional landscaper should be able to explain exactly what they're applying to your lawn, when, and why. Vague answers like "we put down the standard stuff" from someone applying fertilizers or herbicides to your property should concern you.

Questions to Ask Before You Hire

  1. "Can you provide a Certificate of Insurance for general liability and workers' compensation?"
  2. "Is your company GI-BMP certified? Who is the certified applicator?"
  3. "Do you hold a current FDACS pesticide applicator license for Category 4?"
  4. "What specific products do you use for fertilization and weed control, and at what rates?"
  5. "Can I see a sample service agreement for ongoing maintenance?"
  6. "What is your process when you discover a problem on my property between scheduled visits?"
